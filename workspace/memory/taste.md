# Taste — Editorial Reference

Last updated: 2026-04-01

## Principles
(Distilled from everything shared — updated as the library grows)

- **Personal confession + framework = the formula.** The pieces that resonate most combine raw vulnerability with a structured insight. Pure confession is empty. Pure framework is boring. The winners thread a personal story through a conceptual lens.
- **Name the thing people feel but can't articulate.** The viral pieces don't teach new information — they give language to experiences people already have. "Being able to articulate your feelings isn't the same as feeling them." "The limiting factor is no longer intelligence, but desire."
- **Vulnerability progression.** Start safe/aspirational, move toward the harder stuff. Don't lead with the wound.
- **Zeitgeist anchors work.** Tying content to a cultural moment (Eileen Gu, Ezra Klein, a viral tweet) gives shareable entry points. The formula: cultural moment → framework → actionable prompts.
- **Substack audience is ripe.** Journaling, self-development, and therapy-adjacent content performs well. 35M+ monthly actives as of 2026. This is where therapy-fluent people read.
- **"Neuroplasticity" as resonant language.** Science-framing lowers the "is this woo?" barrier while being a trending search term.
- **Pull, don't push.** Values-based marketing. Provide value without asking for return. Teach, don't sell.
- **Oliver Burkeman model:** personal storytelling + life philosophy + therapeutic frameworks + tapped into cultural conversation. The newsletter you actually look forward to receiving.

## Tone Rules
- No effusive openers ("This is brilliant!", "Great article!")
- Skip connective padding ("Here's how this relates...")
- Get straight to the substance
- Go deeper than "this maps to Rosebud" — push toward provocative questions, tensions, and unexplored angles
- Treat shared content as material to think with, not just validate

## Response Format for Shared Content
When an article/video/essay is shared in Slack:
1. **Summary first** — assume others in the channel won't read the full piece. Give them enough to understand it.
2. **Key quotes/excerpts** — pull the lines that land hardest. Let the author's voice come through.
3. **Why it matters** — connect to our taste, strategy, or open questions. Keep this tight.
4. **One or two questions** — push the thinking forward, don't just validate.

The goal: someone who doesn't click the link walks away understanding the piece and why it's relevant.

### "The Human Alignment Problem" — Intimate Mirror (coach), shared by Brandon, 2026-03-31
- **Source:** Substack essay
- **Core argument:** AI alignment is really a human alignment problem. The bottleneck isn't intelligence but the quality of desire behind the prompt. Four modes of AI use: trivial, extractive, contemplative, refusal. The third group (people with interior work) is tiny but the most important.
- **Two-gap framework:** Gap 1 (desire → actualization) collapsing fast. Gap 2 (what we think we want → what we actually want) isn't. The asymmetry is where danger lives.

- **Why it resonated (Brandon):** "A really good articulation of different ways to relate to AI that maps directly to our findings from Who To Serve." Author is a coach who'd be a good practitioner partner lead.
- **Questions raised:** Is Rosebud's real value not giving people what they ask for, but helping them discover what they're actually asking for? What would content for practitioners like this author look like?
- **Tags:** AI + spirituality, desire clarity, practitioner voice, growth vs relief, philosophical

## Emerging Patterns

**The team gravitates toward content that:**
1. Takes a nuanced position on AI + inner work (not "AI good" or "AI bad" but "here's what actually happens when you use it")
2. Is written by practitioners or people with real interior work experience (coaches, therapists, meditators, people in therapy)
3. Blends personal story with conceptual framework
4. Names a tension rather than resolving it neatly
5. Could plausibly be written by someone in Rosebud's therapy-fluent ICP

**Two voices emerging:**
- **Brandon's taste:** personal essays, newsletters you look forward to, vulnerability + philosophy, content experiments (Substack writers trying Rosebud), pull marketing
- **Arjun's taste:** strong product narratives, academic frameworks applied to real life, data-backed storytelling

**The content Rosebud should make lives in the gap between:**
- The viral ChatGPT-ruined-my-life essay (relief-oriented, cautionary) and
- The Human Alignment Problem essay (growth-oriented, aspirational)
- Rosebud's content should be the voice of the person who's done both — who went through the spiral AND came out with a practice.
